About Me

I am Yadav Subba — born on the eighth of September, two thousand and three, in the quiet village of Dikhlem Nepali Subba Gaon, Hawaipur, West Karbi Anglong, Assam. It is the same earth that witnessed my first steps, the same sky that watched me grow, and the same soil that holds the roots of my family. I was raised under the blessings of my parents — my mother, whose quiet strength has been my anchor, and my father, whose memory remains my compass.

My schooling began in the modest classrooms of a local government school. From the first letters I traced to the final examinations I sat for, my education was shaped entirely within the walls of government institutions. There was no coaching centre, no private tuition, no privilege of extra guidance — only the raw, unpolished determination of a boy who believed that effort alone could bridge any gap.

In the summer of 2020, when I was seventeen and a half, my world collapsed. My father — the sole provider of our family — passed away after a fall from a betel nut tree. He was fifty-five years old. He was our strength, our foundation, our everything. His passing left us with nothing but grief, uncertainty, and the weight of survival. My mother, who had never managed finances beyond the household, became the head of our family. My sister and I stood beside her, learning to navigate a world that had suddenly become unfamiliar.

That same year, I appeared for my HSLC examinations. I was recovering from a medical condition, and my preparation had been minimal. Yet I refused to surrender. Every day, I cycled alone to my exam centre at New Hindi High School — pedalling through the Dikhlem Raisem Mailoo road, while my friends rode past me on their bikes and vehicles. I was alone, but I was not defeated. I passed with a third division, and my father, before his passing, smiled with pride. That smile remains one of my most treasured memories.

In the year two thousand and twenty-three, I sat for my Higher Secondary examinations. Without the luxury of coaching or tuition, I achieved eighty point four percent — a result that filled me with quiet pride. I was honoured with the Banikanda Kakati Award under the Pragyan Bharati Scheme, a recognition from the Directorate of Higher Education, Government of Assam. I received a scooty as a reward. It was a moment of triumph — but it was also a moment of longing. I wished, with all my heart, that my father could have witnessed it. I wished he could have told me, "You did well, my son. Keep going."

I have now completed my Bachelor of Arts in English (Honours). My results are awaited, but my journey through language and literature has already shaped me. I write with precision, I read with depth, and I communicate with clarity. My hearing impairment has never been a barrier to my words; it has only made them more deliberate, more thoughtful, more meaningful.

Beyond code and composition, I am a mathematician at heart. I teach mathematics to students from Class 1 to 10. I believe that numbers are not just symbols — they are stories. They are patterns, logic, and beauty. I teach because I believe every child deserves a teacher who can make them fall in love with the language of the universe.

I am a self-taught web developer, a content writer, a teacher, and a survivor. I have walked through grief, cycled through illness, and coded through silence. I have built tools, taught children, and written stories. My words are my strength; my code is my craft. And my purpose is to create — not for recognition, but for meaning.

I am still learning. I am still building. I am still here. And I will not stop until I have made my father proud — even from wherever he is.
